Section 05

How much time does your city actually take from you each week?

We translate the average proximity index for each city into estimated walking minutes, then multiply by 10 weekly trips (5 destinations × there and back). We assume 5 round trips per week to each service, a working commuter's schedule, to illustrate a plausible upper bound. The chart below shows total weekly minutes spent on foot to reach transit, groceries, healthcare, and parks.


Weekly walking minutes to essential services, by city

Hover a bar to see a breakdown by service type. Note: The proximity index is drawn from Statistics Canada's Proximity Measures Database. Each block receives a gravity-model score (0–1) based on the number and size of nearby services weighted by real road-network distance, normalized nationally. We convert the index to walking minutes using each service's defined threshold (e.g. 1 km for groceries, 1.5 km for schools)

Section 07 · Key Takeaways

What did we learn?

The data shows disparities in access to essential services across British Columbia. These differences are influenced by geographic location, and population density, indicating that service distribution is not uniform across the province.

🏙️

Urban areas perform consistently well, but smaller cities can also be accessible

While Vancouver ranks highest overall in service accessibility, the data also reveals that smaller cities such as Victoria and North Vancouver also achieve relatively high scores. This suggests that high accessibility is not exclusive to urban centres, but can also be achieved at smaller scales.

🚗

Suburban areas show lower proximity to services

The data indicates that car-dependent suburban communities tend to have lower proximity scores across service categories. This pattern suggests that residents in these areas are likely to spend more time traveling to access services.

⚖️

Service distribution reflects local priorities

Different regions prioritize different services. For example, Maple Ridge has higher concentrations of employment and educational services, while Whistler has more access to parks and health-related amenities.
